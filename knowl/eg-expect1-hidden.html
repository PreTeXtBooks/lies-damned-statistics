Let’s compute the expectation of net profit RV \(X\) in the silly betting game of Example Example 4.3.2, whose distribution we computed in Example Example 4.3.6. Plugging straight into the definition, we see
\begin{equation*} E(X)=\sum p_i\,x_i = \frac12\cdot5 + \frac12\cdot(-5)=2.5-2.5 = 0 \ . \end{equation*}
In other words, your average net gain playing this silly game many times will be zero. Note that does not mean anything like “if you lose enough times in a row, the chances of starting to win again will go up,” as many gamblers seem to believe, it just means that, in the very long run, we can expect the average winnings to be approximately zero — but no one knows how long that run has to be before the balancing of wins and losses happens.
